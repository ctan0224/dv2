Australia's Black Summer

2019-2020 Australian bushfire season

Where it burned

The 2019-2020 Black Summer was unlike anything Australia had seen before. More than 18 million hectares burned, over 3,000 buildings were destroyed, 34 people lost their lives, and satellites recorded 282,000 fire hotspots across the country. The fires stretched across nearly every corner of the continent—but it was the eastern seaboard, tracing a deep red arc from Queensland down through New South Wales and into Victoria, where the land burned longest and the fires burned hardest.

Sample of 8,000 hotspots. Colour shows fire intensity (MW).

New South Wales was the hardest hit, with nearly 100,000 satellite detections, 2,176 homes lost and 25 lives taken. But the fires told different stories in different places—Western Australia's blazes were fewer yet far more intense, while the Northern Territory lost the most land of all, with 6.8 million hectares burned across its vast interior.

When it burned

What began as an unusually early outbreak in June 2019 gradually grew into one of Australia's most devastating fire seasons. Activity built steadily through winter and spring, then exploded in December and January as a record heatwave gripped the continent. The crisis reached its peak in early January 2020, with detections and intensity climbing to their highest levels. Throughout the season, fires burned across both day and night, though daytime hours consistently saw the greatest number of hotspots.

Why it was so severe

Before a single spark was struck, the land was already in crisis. Months of relentless heat had baked the soil dry, and the rains that should have come in spring never arrived—in some states, more than 65% below what the season normally brings. By the time summer peaked, Australia was recording its second warmest on record, with temperatures running over 2°C above the long-term average across much of the east coast. This deadly combination of prolonged heat and drought left vegetation across the continent primed to burn.

Hotter, drier, more fire

The numbers tell a simple story: where it was hotter and drier, it burned more. NSW sits alone at the top of the scatter—nearly 100,000 hotspots, a rainfall deficit that dwarfed most other states, and temperatures running almost 2°C above normal in the lead-up season. Queensland and the Northern Territory weren't far behind. And when Summer 2020 finally arrived, the bar chart lights up red—the season peaking exactly when and where the climate had been building toward it all year.
